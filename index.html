Fernando Amorsolo






About

Fernando Amorsolo y Cueto (1892-1972) holds the esteemed title of being the Philippines’ first National Artist,
recognized as the “Grand Old Man of Philippine Art.” Renowned for his mastery of luminosity and his ability to depict
the quintessential provincial Filipina, Amorsolo's paintings often portray scenes set in radiant rural landscapes.
His works encapsulate the essence of Filipino culture and heritage, capturing the beauty of everyday life in the Philippines.


Self-Portrait of Fernando Amorsolo

Birth: May 30, 1892; Manila

Death: April 24, 1972; Quezon City
School & Education

In 1909, Amorsolo graduated with honors from the Art School of the Liceo De Manila, marking the beginning
of his artistic journey. He furthered his studies at the UP Diliman College of Fine Arts, where he was mentored by his uncle,
prominent Philippine painter Fabian de la Rosa. After graduating with honors in 1916, he received sponsorship
from Spanish businessman Enrique Zóbel de Ayala to study at the Real Academia de Bellas Artes de San Fernando in Madrid, Spain.
This international exposure greatly influenced his artistic style and technique.
Works & Portrayals

Amorsolo's body of work is vast and diverse, covering various themes and subjects. His paintings often depict
scenes of rural life, including farmers in rice fields, women sorting through produce, and vibrant society portraits.
Notable works include his masterpiece "Rice Planting," which became an iconic representation
of the Commonwealth period. Amorsolo's unique backlighting technique, developed during his career,
became his signature style, infusing his paintings with a luminous quality. He was also a pioneer in portraying
traditional Filipino customs and occupations, capturing scenes such as fishing, market visits, and everyday chores.
Through his extensive contributions, Amorsolo's legacy continues to define and perpetuate a distinct
element of the nation’s artistic and cultural heritage.
